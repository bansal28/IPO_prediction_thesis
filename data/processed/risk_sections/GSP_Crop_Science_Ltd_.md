# **_10. Our business is sensitive to seasonal vagaries and adverse weather conditions which affect the agrochemical industry. Seasonal variations and unfavourable local and global weather patterns may have an adverse effect on our business, results of operations and financial condition._** 

Our business is sensitive to weather conditions which affect the agrochemical industry; such as drought, floods, cyclones and natural disasters, as well as events such as pest infestations. The weather can affect the presence of crop disease and pest infestations in the short term on a regional basis, and accordingly, may adversely affect the demand for our products. Adverse conditions, especially drought conditions, can result in significantly lower than normal crop plantings and yields for our customers and therefore lower demand for our products. This can result in our sales in a 

41 

particular region varying substantially from year to year. Weather conditions can also result in earlier or later plantings and affect the levels of pest infestations, which may affect both the timing and volume of our sales or the product mix. Adverse weather conditions may also cause volatility in the prices of commodities, which may affect farmers’ decisions about the types and quantum of crops to plant and may consequently affect the sales of our products. As per the ICRA Report, one of the primary concerns for the global agriculture sector is climate change, which results in extreme weather phenomena and altered climatic patterns that significantly affect crop production. The increasing concern over climate change may also result in enhanced regional and global legal and regulatory requirements to reduce or mitigate the effects of greenhouse gases, as well as more stringent regulation of water rights. In the event that such regulations are enacted and are more aggressive than the sustainability measures that we are currently undertaking to monitor our emissions, improve our energy efficiency, and reduce and reuse water, we may experience significant increases in our costs of operations. While our business has not been materially impacted by any such adverse climatic conditions in the six months ended September 30, 2025, and Fiscals 2025, 2024, and 2023, there can be no assurance that such instances will not occur in the future. 

Further, the sale of products is seasonal on account of monsoon season; with the demand for fungicides, herbicides, insecticides and plant growth regulators being generally higher during the monsoon season in India and other jurisdictions where our products are exported. As a result, our Company typically generates higher revenue during the first six months in a Fiscal on account of reliance on ‘Kharif’ cropping cycle, as Kharif crops are sown at the onset of the monsoons and harvested at the end of the rainy season, aligning with the Indian subcontinent's southwest monsoon _(Source: ICRA Report)_ . Therefore, poor rainfall in monsoon season in a particular year may result in the decline in demand for our products. Consequently, excess rainfall in the monsoon season in a particular year may also result in a decline in sales of our products due to crop destruction. While our business has not been materially impacted by shortfall or excess rainfall in monsoons in the six months ended September 30, 2025, and Fiscals 2025, 2024, and 2023, there can be no assurance that such instances will not occur in the future. 

As a result of the abovementioned seasonal fluctuations, our sales and results of operations may vary in each Fiscal and may not be relied upon as indicators of the sales or results of operations of other Fiscals, or of our future performance. Any significant reduction in the area under cultivation in Kharif crops may significantly reduce the demand for our products. Further, the demand for our products is dependent on the cropping pattern which may vary year on year for these Kharif crops. Any significant changes in the cultivable area and the cropping pattern for Kharif crops may impact our sales and profitability.

As per the ICRA Report, the Indian insecticide market is subject to the following threats: (i) The Emergence of Pest Resistance: A major challenge in the domestic insecticide market is the rise of pest resistance, thereby hindering effective pest control. Insects may develop metabolic resistance due to sustained exposure to pesticides, which could lead to a rapid rise in the growth of harmful insects and hinder pest management strategies; (ii) Environmental and Health Concerns: Insecticides can pose risks to human health, potentially causing symptoms such as eye irritation, coughing, heart problems, and breathing difficulties upon ingestion, inhalation, or skin absorption. Fear regarding the side-effects of chemical insecticides has led the Indian government to increasingly advocate for sustainable agriculture practices, and imposing restrictions on hazardous chemicals. Such action by the government could constrain growth in the agrochemical market; and (iii) Regulatory Hurdles: Obtaining approval for new insecticide products in India is a time-consuming, expensive and rigorous process that requires extensive assessments of safety, effectiveness, and environmental impact of the insecticide. This lengthy approval process can lead to a delay in the market entry and increase costs significantly, thereby hampering market expansion.

As per the ICRA Report, the global fungicide market is also prone to various threats and challenges, which, if not addressed could lead to a decline in the growth of global fungicide market. These include (i) Risk of surpassing Maximum Residue Levels (MRLs): Foreign and domestic regulatory bodies establish limits for MRLs, i.e., the legally permissible maximum residue that is permitted in food products. Failure to comply with MRL regulations can result in substantial financial and legal repercussions for producers, exporters, and businesses in the agrochemical industry; (ii) Compliance costs: Farmers who exceed MRLs may face large penalties, and their produce might be rejected by domestic and export markets, negatively affecting their profitability; (iii) Resistance to agrochemicals: Over the years, the continued use of same type of fungicides has led to an increase in fungal resistance, rendering many traditional chemical solutions less effective and (iv) Reluctance to good agriculture practices: New disease variants that are resistant to fungicides are a common threat faced by farmers around the world. Due to economic pressures to maximize short-term profits, some growers are hesitant to fully adopt good agriculture practices like controlled fungicide application within recommended dosage limits and crop rotation, which may lead to a decline in the efficacy of the fungicide. 

Further, as per the ICRA Report, the Indian fungicide industry is also subject to various restraints, such as: (i) Environmental pollution and health risks: Fungicides raise environmental pollution and health risks. Contamination of water bodies through runoff and leaching from agricultural activities presents a serious environmental challenge that could potentially harm non-target organisms, including beneficial microbes and wildlife; (ii) Fungicide effectiveness: Low application rates of fungicides may fail to provide sufficient protection to crops, while some fungicides may have a limited effect on certain pathogens. Further, incorrect application of fungicides could lead to suboptimal results; (iii) Fungicide Resistance: The emergence of fungicide resistance in plant pathogens is a significant challenge to effective crop protection, affecting the performance of various site-specific fungicide groups.

These difficulties cause delays in product registration and present considerable barriers for companies seeking to launch new PGR products in the market. The rising costs associated with product development and the prolonged approval process result in increased retail prices, ultimately reducing net profit margins for growers and impeding PGR growth; (ii) Lack of awareness: A significant barrier to the adoption of PGRs is the limited awareness among growers regarding their benefits, correct application methods, and cost-saving advantages, leading to their underutilization. This knowledge gap is particularly noticeable in developing regions, limiting knowledge exchange about sustainable PGR use. The prevalence of alternative products like fertilizers and the persistence of traditional farming methods also impede wider PGR adoption. The Indian Plant Growth Regulator market is also susceptible to various restraints such as (i) insufficient awareness among Indian farmers: A major obstacle to the advancement of the PGR market in India is the prevalent lack of awareness among farmers, especially those residing in rural regions. Numerous farmers are not informed about the different types of PGRs available and the advantages they can offer; and (ii) residual toxicity: The presence of PGR residues can lead to environmental contamination and pose risks of foodborne illness.

# **_62. The increasing adoption of non-chemical-based agricultural methods and genetically modified (GM) crop varieties may reduce the demand for our traditional agrochemical products and adversely affect our business, financial condition, and results of operations._** 

A shift towards non-chemical-based agricultural methods poses several risks to traditional agrochemical manufacturers like our Company. As more farmers adopt these methods, the demand for traditional agrochemicals may decline. This decline can lead to a reduced market share for agrochemical manufacturers, impacting their revenue and profitability. The rise of non-chemical practices also puts pressure on agrochemical companies to innovate and develop new products that align with sustainable farming methods, and a failure to adapt could result in a loss of competitiveness. Furthermore, methods emphasizing environmental sustainability may lead to increased regulatory scrutiny of chemical products, potentially resulting in stricter regulations on chemical inputs and new compliance challenges for manufacturers. Growing consumer awareness and preference for organic and sustainably produced food can also diminish the appeal of chemically treated crops, leading to decreased sales for agrochemical manufacturers. The increasing popularity of biopesticides and organic fertilizers is due to their sustainable nature, appealing to environmentally conscious consumers and regulatory bodies advocating for greener agriculture practices. As farmers transition to non-chemical methods, the supply chain for traditional agrochemicals may face disruptions, potentially leading to excess inventory and increased costs for manufacturers. 

Additionally, the increasing adoption of genetically modified (GM) crop varieties is transforming agriculture practices and influencing the demand for certain agrochemicals. Advancements in biotechnology enable the development of crops with enhanced traits, including resistance to pests and diseases, thereby providing better crop yields. This shift in agricultural practices driven by GM crops could alter the demand landscape for various types of agrochemicals, potentially reducing the need for some of our existing products and requiring us to develop or acquire new products compatible with prevalent GM technologies. 

The combined impact of a decline in the use of traditional agrochemicals due to the adoption of non-chemical methods and the potential shift in demand patterns resulting from the increased cultivation of GM crops could lead to reduced sales, increased costs, and necessitate significant investments in research and development to adapt our product portfolio. These factors could have a material adverse effect on our business, financial condition, and results of operations.
